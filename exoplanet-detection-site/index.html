Section 01

Why do we care?

Each new planet challenges our picture of how planetary systems form, migrate and survive.

Detection is only the first step. After discovery, we try to infer mass, radius, density, orbit, atmosphere and possible habitability.

01

Is there a planet at all, or is the signal caused by stellar activity or instrumental noise?

02

Orbit

Period, semi-major axis, eccentricity and inclination tell us how the planet moves.

03

Size + Mass

Transit depth gives radius; radial velocity gives a minimum mass.

04

Atmosphere

Spectroscopy can reveal gases, clouds, temperature structure and habitability clues.

Section 02

From suspicion to discovery

Before 1990s

Claims existed, but precision was not enough. Many early astrometric claims were rejected.

1992

The first confirmed exoplanets were found around pulsar PSR B1257+12 using timing residuals.

1995

51 Pegasi b became the first confirmed planet around a Sun-like star: a surprising Hot Jupiter.

Now

Thousands of exoplanets are known, and multiple detection methods are often combined.

“The trick is not always to see the planet. The trick is to see what the planet does to the star, the light, or the timing.”

Section 04

Transit method

When a planet crosses the stellar disk, the observed brightness dips.

Transit depth ΔF / F ≈ (Rp / R★)²

A bigger planet blocks more light. Repeated dips reveal the orbital period.

Section 05

Radial velocity

The planet and star orbit their common barycentre. The star's line-of-sight velocity creates a periodic Doppler shift.

Simplified idea red shift ↔ moving away · blue shift ↔ moving toward

RV curves can reveal period, eccentricity and minimum mass Mp sin i.

Section 06

Wobble family: pulsar timing + astrometry

Pulsar Timing

Pulsars behave like ultra-precise clocks. A planet makes the pulsar move around the barycentre, so pulses arrive slightly early or late.

timing residual = observed arrival − predicted arrival

Astrometry

Instead of measuring speed toward us, astrometry measures the tiny sideways motion of the star on the sky.

Section 07

Direct imaging

This is the most intuitive method and one of the hardest.

Planets are faint and close to extremely bright stars. Instruments use coronagraphs, adaptive optics, infrared imaging and post-processing to suppress the glare.

Reflected-light contrast Fp/F★ = p(λ)(Rp/a)²g(α)
star blocked
Look for faint points after subtracting the star.
Section 08

Gravitational microlensing

A foreground star bends and magnifies light from a background star. A planet can create a short extra bump.

This method can detect planets at large distances and does not require the host star to be bright, but events are rare and usually non-repeatable.

Section 10

The future: better instruments + better algorithms

Precision photometry

Cleaner light curves help us detect smaller transits and improve atmospheric follow-up.

Extreme adaptive optics

Sharper imaging reduces atmospheric distortion and separates planets from stellar glare.

Spectroscopy

Absorption and emission features can reveal atmospheric chemistry and temperature.

Machine learning

ML can classify candidates, remove systematics and search large mission archives.

The goal is shifting from “is there a planet?” to “what kind of world is it?”
